Notes

Please allow me to be self-indulgent. This has been a long project and I've earned it.

What started as a project about the Green New Deal and how it was developing in Canada turned quickly into a long-term story about how we all rely on the oil and gas industry more than we think. It was reported over a year through wild events (COVID-19, I’m looking at you) that I could never have planned for. It took hundreds of hours of planning, reporting, writing and coding to bring together.

I’m exhausted.

My ticket to the Green New Deal rally in Washington D.C.

But this project was also an incredible experience that will shape the kind of journalist I become. I learned about another side of Canada with which I have very little experience, and an industry that makes my head spin. I've long said that energy reporting is one of the most difficult beats for new reporters to learn, because it straddles so many fields: economics, politics, chemistry, biology... The list keeps going. I'm immensely thankful to start inching my way towards full understanding of Canada's relationship with oil.

Two moments stand out as favourites while reporting this project.

Montréal was one. I left for the march on a whim, and relentlessly tracked key figures through half a million people in the streets of one of my favourite cities on earth. I ran for blocks and blocks through thick crowds, happened upon Trudeau accidentally as I looked for Greta Thunberg, and made my way to the front of the march to join the legions of photographers looking for a shot of the Swedish teen. I pushed through them all to get the shot of Thunberg at the front, and felt time stop when I knew I had a shot I’ll always remember. In a way, this day was a culmination of all the skills I’ve learned in j-school the past two years.

My time in Lloydminster was the second. I’ve never been driven to ask so many questions, both of others and of myself, than after my trip there. Growing up in BC and living in big cities outside Alberta for my entire life, issues affecting oil and gas workers and how we process this resources are abstract issues to me. Exploring the city and talking to the people there helped me understand what I thought was a no-brainer in a more nuanced way.

I wrote every line of code for this project from scratch. No CMS. People regularly asked me why. I believe journalism owes a lot to good design and technology. Journalists have long known that the way you write or the way you photograph your stories matters - why don’t we put more care into how stories are arranged on the screen? I think this project is, in part, an answer to that question.
