Reporter's Notes

A city in trouble,
a changing climate,
and Canada's tightrope walk to fix both.

By Dexter McMillan


Our glaciers are receding.

Our arctic ice is melting.

Our cities are flooding.

Canada is among the countries hit hardest by climate change.

Our country’s north is experiencing a warming of average temperatures double that of the rest of the world.

But we are not simply victims.

Canada has the world's third largest crude oil reserves.

They account for more than 12 per cent of all our exported goods.

Our greenhouse gas emissions exceed 720 megatons, making us one of the top ten emitters on the globe.

While Canadians fight amongst themselves...

...as oil prices flag and other countries move on from fossil fuels...

...one city waits for help.

t's hard not to call Lloydminster a ghost town, particularly in the summer of 2019. Brand new housing developments languish, half-finished in the dusty prairie grass on the outskirts of the city. Stories of so many other abandoned oil boom towns in Alberta linger like cobwebs in the air.

The Heavy Oil Science Centre is just a few minutes off Highway 17, an arterial that runs through the middle of town and serves as the provincial border between Alberta and Saskatchewan. There is a quiet agreement to keep the Alberta clock, regardless of which side of the road you’re on, making cell phone time — which relies on GPS location instead of human arrangements — a constant frustration.

Squirt the anthropomorphic oil drop also helps users navigate the website for the Heavy Oil Science Centre. Dexter McMillan/May 17, 2019

The Centre feels very much like the beating heart of the community. For residents, the colourful exhibits are a point of pride. For out-of-towners, there is no better place to learn about the city’s history processing heavy oil. It’s also a popular field trip among the schoolchildren of Lloydminster: the Centre’s mascot Squirt - a beaming, anthropomorphic drop of oil - explains the history and science of heavy oil in the region with kid-focused games, exhibits and activities.

In the city – simply called “Lloyd” by locals – fortunes live and die on the rollercoaster of the oil economy, like many prairie towns that are intimately connected to the fossil fuel industry. It is a case study of Canada’s reliance on fossil fuels, and residents of the town have summoned angry politicians to push for a pipeline they believe is critical to restoring Lloydminster — and Alberta — to its former glory. The Canadian government, on the other hand, has promised a carbon tax, and in its quest for appeasement, have satisfied few.

Lloydminster is a city of just over 31,000, but it's hard to stop the word “town” from falling out of your mouth when you describe it. The oil industry workers seem tightly connected to the community. There’s kinship on every corner, from the Husky-sponsored baseball diamond to the community centre slash Heavy Oil Science Centre slash parking lot farmer’s market.

Vic Juba, known around town as “Mr. Lloydminster”, is a big-time oil man in this big-time oil town, and his industry resumé is thicker than Lloydminster crude. Juba spent his entire career at Husky. His reputation has earned him more than half a dozen awards for public service and a monument in the southwest of the city: the Vic Juba Community Theatre.

“I've had a dream career,” said Juba.

Juba began at Husky in 1953 as a technician in the lab. He says at the time, Husky and other oil companies in Canada were struggling to unlock the potential in heavy crude. Getting it to flow through pipelines was the hardest part.

“It was quite an experience to try and determine how much dilatant we have to put in to make it as viscous as possible, [but still allow it to] flow,” said Juba, “not make it 100 miles and then stop dead because of too much friction."

Vic Juba stands in the Heavy Oil Science Centre, which he helped to produce. Dexter McMillan/May 17, 2019

Tourism material lists Lloydminster as the “Heavy Oil Capital of the World." It sits just south of the Cold Lake oil sands and boasts its own heavy oil deposits. But refining heavy oil into more useful products is how Lloydminster truly made its name.

Heavy oil is not the same as other kinds of petroleum. It is thick, difficult to pull out of the ground and needs to be heavily refined before use.

The Husky upgrader in Lloydminster is hard to miss. It's just off the highway that brings you through town. Dexter McMillan/May 17, 2019
Husky donates money to build community spaces like this baseball field. Dexter McMillan/May 17, 2019

Because it is relatively more costly to extract and process heavy oil, the world’s interest has primarily been on conventional oil reserves with a lower viscosity.

Husky’s Lloydminster refinery, built in 1946, essentially unlocked the heavy oil reserves in the surrounding area for use by introducing hydrogen into the oil, turning it into a more valuable product. Husky’s Lloydminster Upgrader, built in 1992 to further process the heavy crude found in the Lloydminster area before shipping to other parts of North America, added further value to this process.

“The asphalt quality is basically the best in the world,” boasts Juba.

The Upgrader turns thick oil like Lloydminster crude into synthetic crude, a more useful product that can be transported by pipeline.

“Basically, heavy oil has too much carbon, and not enough hydrogen,” said Darrell Howell, an engineer and the Upgrader’s first manager.

The Upgrader is a major employer in Lloydminster, providing around 800 jobs. The facility takes heavy crude oil from the surrounding area and processes 29,000 barrels into asphalt, synthetic crude oil and other products every day.

There are five other upgraders in Canada with a combined output of just under 1,500,000 barrels per day. The Lloydminster Upgrader, the only facility not located in Alberta, is just five kilometres away from the provincial border.

In 1988, Juba became Husky’s community liaison, a role of particular importance given the intimate place Husky has in the community. Husky leases approximately 2 million acres of land in the Lloydminster area, making it the largest operator in the area. It makes regular contributions to community spaces like parks and recreational facilities in Lloydminster.

Husky’s scope 1 emissions are listed as 10.2 megatons of CO2 equivalent. Husky is responsible for 1.4 per cent of Canada's overall emissions, and the Lloydminster Upgrader is responsible for 10.6 per cent of Husky’s emissions.

Lloydminster, like other towns and cities in Alberta that sprang up to support oil workers in the 20th century, has an up-and-down economic history.

Corine Price grew up close to Lloydminster before moving away to attend university. She came back to Lloydminster as the town’s archivist in the early 1990s. In the archive room – another of the many civic institutions that co-exist in the same building as the Heavy Oil Science Centre – she sits surrounded by boxes, papers and books. She sips tea with another resident who volunteers with her.

"It seems like the only thing I remember is having drought."

- Corine Price, archivist

“It seemed to me like the city was always growing,” says Price about Lloydminster in the early 2000s. “I remember as a kid, Lloyd wasn’t a city but more like a big town. Now it seems like more of a fast-moving, urban centre."

She said that in 2015, it all started to change.

“That’s when people stopped spending money so frivolously on toys,” she says. “There were a lot of layoffs in the oil field. We started seeing a lot of housing foreclosures and businesses closing."

Many of the newer parts of the city began construction in 2013 at a time when oil had recovered from the 2008 recession and climbed to over $100 USD/barrel, fuelling development and creating jobs in the city. But by January 2015, the price of oil had plummeted almost to $52 USD/barrel, stalling out the economy. New houses under construction were stopped almost overnight.

During lean times like this, Price says austerity becomes as intimately connected to the city as heavy oil.

“You feel it everywhere."

Juba says that in those tougher times, mental health services were flooded with people. “We were finding mental health issues very prevalent,” he said. “I remember this one lady I talked to. She said that the best she could do was an appointment in six month’s time. I got a problem today, not six months from now.”

Once again, Husky is having trouble in Alberta. Shortly after the 2019 federal election, the company laid off almost 400 workers in its Calgary office, citing Alberta’s oil curtailment policies as the reason. Despite curtailment, the price of Western Canadian Select (WCS), the Canadian benchmark oil product, was just under $38 USD/barrel before COVID-19 and a price war between Russia and Saudi Arabia tanked the price of oil all over the world.

Now, Western Canadian Select sits at just over $6 a barrel. Husky’s 2019 annual report (published just before the COVID-19 pandemic) cites "infrastructure constraints” as the cause of declining prices for oil products impacting its operations.

They cannot get their product to Canadian ports for export around the world.

In other words: They need a pipeline.

etting oil from point A to point B through days of challenging terrain has always been difficult for Canada. As a result, we’re no strangers to pipeline battles. Youth and Indigenous leaders have been at the forefront of many.

Wet’suwet’en protesters clashed with police in Northern British Columbia in January 2019, and again a year later, after construction crews – backed by RCMP officers – moved through Wet’suwet’en territory without consent from Indigenous leadership. The crews were building TransCanada’s Liquid Natural Gas (LNG) pipeline.

Activists have also opposed TransCanada’s Energy East pipeline – a proposal which would have run 1.1 million barrels of crude oil per year through a pipeline from Alberta to Canada’s east coast ports in New Brunswick. It failed after strong opposition to expanded oil and gas resources, and reports that former Québec Premier Jean Charest provided consultation to the project despite being on the payroll of TransCanada.

The climate strike in Montréal had thousands coming out to protest the Canadian government's inaction on climate change. Dexter McMillan/Sept. 27, 2019
A young Canadian holds up a sign at the Montréal protests. Dexter McMillan/Sept. 27, 2019

But the current fractures in Canada run along the fault line that is the Trans Mountain pipeline expansion. The pipeline was once owned and managed by Kinder Morgan, an oil company headquartered in Houston, Texas. The then-Liberal majority government, headed by Prime Minister Justin Trudeau, purchased the project in 2018 from a hesitant Kinder Morgan for $4.5 billion to ensure it would be built. The project was slated to increase the pipeline’s capacity to move oil along most of its 1,150-kilometre route from Burnaby, B.C., to near Edmonton, Alta., increasing capacity from 300,000 barrels per day to almost 900,000. This almost-tripling of capacity would mean that crude oil could be shipped from Alberta to coastal Canadian ports, where it could be sold and shipped to foreign markets at a higher volume.

The National Energy Board (NEB), a national watchdog that reviews energy infrastructure projects, ruled in favour of the Trans Mountain pipeline expansion in February 2019, clearing the way for the government to proceed with construction. The Trans Mountain Expansion website predicted the project could employ 15,000 people for construction alone, and provide “the equivalent of 37,000 direct, indirect and induced jobs per year during operations.”

Opposition to the latest oil infrastructure project, the Trans Mountain expansion, is strong. These tensions made the Montréal climate march on September 27, 2019, the largest climate demonstration in Canada yet.

Many youth at the Montréal march said they have lost faith in the ability of politicians to take action. They said it’s hypocritical for Trudeau to be building a pipeline while claiming to be a climate leader.

“I’m not here to deliver a message of hope, but a message of urgency,” said Elijah Concepcion (not pictured). He’s barely keeping himself from yelling, instead carefully choosing his words and hitting each of them with an emotional punch.

Hear audio from the march.

Here, students at Université du Québec à Montréal prepare signs for the second week of global climate strikes, culminating in the march. Flyers posted on bulletin boards and concrete columns through the university and similar posters seen through the city served as a rallying cry for students to participate in what would be the largest climate demonstration in Canada.

The French-language university UQAM — along with the majority of other schools in Montréal — cancelled classes so students could attend the strike.

"Bienvenue à Montréal," read a poster of Greta Thunberg in the halls of UQAM. "Avec toi, Greta, pour la grève du climat."

Youth across the globe have been galvanized into action by Indigenous activists like Autumn Peltier here in Canada and international sensation Greta Thunberg. Both led the march in Montréal.

It's estimated that more than half a million people attended Montréal’s march in one of Canada's most populous cities.

Demonstrations have not been limited to the one in Montréal. They have been sprouting up all across Canada last year. This rally, held just adjacent to downtown Vancouver, had guest speakers, a choir singing songs about Trudeau and no shortage of angry signs.

“Please know that your work to protect the environment pushes me and pushes our party every single day,” said former Environment Minister Catherine McKenna soon after her re-election in October 2019.

Her message has been ridiculed by activists in Canada as typical of the Trudeau government’s compromise between those among record-breaking crowds in Montréal and the oil and gas industry.

Behind a frosted glass door in Lloydminster’s city hall building, tucked just into the Alberta side of the provincial border, Mayor Gerald Aalbers sits in his office. Next to him is a shelf of prairie trinkets: A golden oil derrick that bobs up and down to music, a Sir John A. MacDonald bobblehead and a souvenir baseball.

He is a jovial man with the beaming smile of a politician, and he’s crystal clear about why he ran for mayor of Lloydminster in 2016: “I was angry."

Several trinkets sit on a shelf in the corner of Mayor Gerald Aalbers's office. Dexter McMillan/May 16, 2019
Gerald Aalbers has been the mayor of Lloydminster since 2016. Dexter McMillan/May 16, 2019

2016 was a bad year for Lloydminster, and Aalbers, like the rest of the city, was feeling pinched by soaring unemployment.

The spectre of the 1980s finds its way into our conversation like it finds its way into many conversations in this city. During that decade, unemployment spiked to record levels in Alberta, when the price of oil crashed and at its peak, left 12.8 per cent of Albertans out of work and looking for jobs.

Aalbers describes how a Husky refinery expansion project saved Lloydminster from that decade’s recession.

“The new technology increased [production] by four,” he said with some measure of pride. “The people that were on unemployment now have work 365 days of the year...Our unemployment rate dropped dramatically.”

This decade, the Trans Mountain pipeline project is a similar stitch, running along the border of the increasingly anxious, oil-dependent province of Alberta.

“A pipeline is the safest methodology we have today to move oil,” says Aalbers, describing the need for a pipeline project like the Trans Mountain expansion. He says that companies are seeing fewer and fewer profits because of difficulties moving the oil. “If you can't sell your product…we've really created a roadblock in the system.”

The Liberal goverment is assuring Albertans that they intend to follow through with the project.

“It must be built and it will be built,” Finance Minister Bill Morneau told reporters shortly after Trudeau purchased the project from Kinder Morgan for $4.5 billion.

After the 2019 election, more seats are in favour of building the pipeline than not. Trudeau's Liberal Party and the Conservative Party formed a majority voting bloc on the issue. Parties who oppose the project do not have enough votes to contest.

“It gives the Liberals a talking point even if it's not effective in swaying Alberta opinion,” said Jon Pammett, a political science professor at Carleton University. “It kind of gives them credibility in that national unity issue while maybe endangering the environmental one.”

Unfortunately for Trudeau’s Liberals, the commitment to the Trans Mountain expansion seems to have galvanized climate protesters and has done nothing to assure Albertans that it would ever actually be built.

“He’s not pro-pipeline,” said Jim Spenrath, a Lloydminster resident I met alongside a handful of others at a restaurant in Lloydminster. He’s worked in the oil and gas industry for more than four decades. “Not a bit.”

Another Lloydminster resident, Richard Clinger, scoffs when asked about Trudeau’s purchase of the pipeline. “I think he bought it so he could sit on it.”

Rosemarie Falk, the Conservative MP representing Clinger and Spenrath’s riding of Lloydminster-Battlefords, echoes Clinger’s lack of enthusiasm for the pipeline purchase.

“I believe it’s just to show oil and gas workers, ‘Look, we care. We bought a pipeline,’” she said. “’But we’re not doing anything with it.’”

Shortly after speaking to Aalbers, Spenrath and Clinger, the dust was settling on Alberta’s provincial election. The United Conservative Party's Jason Kenney swept the NDP out of Alberta legislature in April 2019 on fighting words:

Bring back our jobs.

ome North Americans believe jobs in oil and gas are never coming back. Organizers in countries around the world are pushing for a Green New Deal, which is a sweeping plan to shift away from dependence on fossil fuels without putting millions out of work by transitioning to a green economy.

Congressperson Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez at the Green New Deal Rally in Washington, D.C. Dexter McMillan/May 13, 2019

In the United States, the Green New Deal is championed by Congressperson Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez in Congress and modelled after Franklin Delano Roosevelt’s original New Deal, the Green New Deal calls for extensive reforms and a significant financial stimulus to create jobs for oil and gas workers in renewable energy. The idea is to bridge the gap between our faltering present and our questionable future, with jobs as the primary building blocks.

Environmental groups in Canada see the potential in her idea and have called on the government to adopt a similar plan as the best way to mend a fractured country on a broken planet.

Listen to an explainer podcast to learn what the Green New Deal is, what it's calling for, and where the Canadian movement is at today.

Dylan Penner, a campaigner who is part of the planning of Canada’s Green New Deal, says we “don't have a moment to lose” in implementing a Green New Deal here in Canada.

But in the United States, where the movement began, it is well underway. Ocasio-Cortez and others drafted and passed a resolution in Congress titled "Recognizing the duty of the federal government to create a Green New Deal." For the first half of 2019, politicians and organizers began touring to drum up support for the movement and push it from a non-binding resolution to a full-fledged bill.

Outside Howard University's Cramton Auditorium in Washington, D.C., hundreds begin gathering for a Green New Deal rally, the last in a cross-country tour.

The tour drew thousands of attendees across the country. The Sunrise Movement and other climate activists brought guest speakers to each spot.

There were rumours circulating beforehand that Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez — the Democratic Congressional Representative for the 14th District of New York — would be taking the stage.

But first, Varshini Prakesh, a leader in the Sunrise Movement, took the stage to hype up the crowd. Prakesh has been organizing Green New Deal rallies all over the United States, and has emerged as a key figure in the plan alongside Ocasio-Cortez.

Finally, Ocasio-Cortez took the stage at the end of the evening with a fiery speech aiming to drum up support for a Green New Deal. At the end of her speech, the crowd erupted.

A year later, the COVID-19 pandemic and oil price war resulting in massive job losses in the industry has made the movement more relevant than ever.

Senator Ed Markey and Senator Bernie Sanders were also in attendance to support the movement and the resolution, tabled in the US Congress in 2018. Sanders promised a Green New Deal in a run for Democratic party presidential nominee before losing the nomination to Joe Biden.

Shortly after AOC took the stage in Washington, environmental groups in Canada saw the potential in the idea. Together they formed the Pact for a Green New Deal, and called on the Canadian government to adopt a similar plan as an alternative to the Trans Mountain pipeline expansion project and amid simmering tensions with First Nations along the pipeline route. They held town halls across Canada to hear what people wanted to see in a Green New Deal. They found interest in a program that cuts emissions in half, while creating jobs in green technology and respecting Indigenous sovereignty.

“The world is finally waking up,” said Penner. “We're working to ensure 2020 is going to be the year of the Green New Deal.”

Green New Deal is evidently not the Liberal Party’s chosen path to a sustainable future. Their two-pronged approach consists of a pipeline for Alberta and a carbon tax to keep carbon emissions under control and protect our environment from rising temperatures.

A carbon tax is a mandatory levy applied at the provincial level charged to organizations on carbon emissions. The federal government’s proposal starts the tax at $10 per tonne of emissions and it will be increased annually to a maximum of $50 per tonne by 2022, according to the Liberal Party’s website.

Prime Minister Justin Trudeau marches with his family in the Montréal climate strike shortly before the Canadian federal election. Surrounding him, marchers protest the pipeline he nationalized. Dexter McMillan/September 27, 2019

But the carbon tax has a wave of conservative premiers fuming. Reconciling the growing divide between many Albertans and the rest of Canada may prove to be more difficult than simply dangling a pipeline along with a carbon tax.

To complicate matters for the federal government, provincial leadership has taken a decidedly blue tinge in the past two years. Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Alberta and Ontario all elected conservative premiers, many running on a populist, anti-carbon tax message.

All of these premiers say that the tax will make life more expensive for Canadian consumers. Alberta premier Jason Kenney has vowed to fight the tax and Saskatchewan Premier Scott Moe is taking Trudeau’s carbon tax to court. In an op-ed in the Financial Post, he argues that the carbon tax is unconstitutional and will not “significantly reduce emissions,” a statement that is incompatible with the Nobel Prize-winning work of William Nordhaus and Paul Romer, who in 2018 proved the efficacy of such a tax if increased over time.

Ontario premier Doug Ford has already taken the carbon tax to court and lost. In June 2019, the Ontario Court of Appeal ruled that the carbon tax imposed on provincial governments was constitutional.

“The need for a collective approach to a matter of national concern…permits Canada to adopt minimum national standards to reduce GHG emissions,” read the decision from Chief Justice of Ontario George Strathy.

Thanks to the work of Nordhaus and Romer, we know that a carbon tax is one of the most effective measures we have to combat climate change. British Columbia has had a carbon tax since 2008. It was increased in April 2019 to $40 per tonne of CO2 equivalent, and is providing rebates of $150 per adult to offset the increasing cost to consumers.

Trudeau for his part has committed to providing rebates to offset these costs. But in Lloydminster, there is considerable anxiety about the tax.

"We’ve wasted decades – years and years – fucking up our planet while our leaders are greenwashing their speeches. I'm tired."

- Elijah Concepcion, student and Montréal march attendee

“The carbon tax feels very much like a penalty,” said Spenrath. “It’s a tax grab at this point because they’re not redirecting that money anywhere.”

Spenrath’s point gets vigorous head-nods from others around the table. But a Pembina Institute report suggests that more than $1 billion collected from the carbon tax in Alberta during fiscal year 2017-18 has been reinvested into the economy. Furthermore, in 2018, it only cost Albertans about $5 more per month and 60 per cent of them received a full rebate.

Reports indicate, however, that Canada would need at least a $70 per tonne tax to meet Paris targets, meaning the controversial Canadian carbon tax may not even be enough.

s Canada aims to ramp up export of petroleum around the world, even as oil prices plummet, other countries are moving away from burning fossil fuels for energy. Several countries in Scandinavia have taken innovative approaches to both energy generation and planning for a future without hydrocarbons, including one of the most aggressive carbon taxes in the world.

Sweden’s carbon tax is currently at CAD $162 per tonne of CO2 equivalent, compared to just $10 per tonne in Canada established by the Liberals. Taxes have been levied on motor fuels since the 1930s, but the current carbon tax was introduced in 1991 and set at CAD $35 per tonne. Over decades, it’s been gradually increased to give corporate emitters and individuals time to adjust. It has also come alongside a decrease in income taxes, effectively shifting priorities without hitting consumers too hard.

Despite Ontario premier Doug Ford’s claim last year that a carbon tax would trigger a recession, the Swedish tax has not stalled out the country’s economy. Between implementation in 1991 and 2017, the economy has steadily grown.

Swedish climate activist Greta Thunberg is depicted here in street art "saving the world" in Teufelsberg, Germany, on the outskirts of Berlin. Dexter McMillan/December 28, 2019

The Norwegians have also elected for a different way to manage their natural oil resources. They have been exporting oil since their discovery of oil in the North Sea in 1969. The federal bank of Norway set up Oljefondets – Norwegian for “The Oil Fund” – that has generated 4.9 billion Norwegian kroner (or CAD $719 million) through investments in businesses and real estate around the world since 1998.

“The aim of the oil fund is to ensure responsible and long-term management of revenue from Norway’s oil and gas resources in the North Sea so that this wealth benefits both current and future generations,” reads the fund’s website.

Norway and Alberta both tax oil revenues through royalties on oil pulled out of the ground. Norway has taxed companies at a much higher rate. The Canadian Centre for Policy Alternatives, a Canadian think tank, reports that Norway retains 85 per cent of oil revenues, while Alberta retains just seven per cent.

More importantly, Norway’s focus on a future without oil means they prioritized saving and investing the money. Alberta put most oil earnings into general revenues to balance the budget, withholding some for the Alberta Heritage Savings Fund, their version of Oljefondets. The idea was to make Alberta a friendly, low-tax place for businesses to boom.

Amager Bakke is tucked right along the coastline just east of Copenhagen's city centre. Dexter McMillan/January 6, 2020

The Alberta Heritage Savings Fund is currently valued at CAD $18 billion, just 1.4 per cent of the CAD $1.3 trillion Norwegian fund’s value.

Meanwhile, elsewhere in Scandinavia, governments are shifting demand for fossil fuels entirely. Copenhagen, Denmark has been burning their garbage as a source of energy for almost five decades.

Amager Bakke, a brand-new waste-to-energy plant, opened in 2017. It converts every tonne of garbage into 2700 kWh of heating for 150,000 homes in the surrounding area and a small amount of electrical energy.

Canada burns garbage for heat at only a few sites across the country. There is some disagreement about whether waste-to-heat generation is actually sustainable in Canada and whether we should be expanding our capacity to burn garbage, a limited resource that society is working harder and harder to reduce the supply of. Regardless, waste-to-energy plants like Amager Bakke in Copenhagen displace some of the country's demand for oil and gas.

Amager Bakke receives 250-300 truckloads of trash every day, half from businesses in the surrounding area and half from Copenhagen homes. In this room, an operator will sit and monitor several mechanized claws that move the fuel to an incinerator. The claws are fully automated.

“This facility is a bit too big compared to how much we need in the Copenhagen area,” said Sune Scheibye, media consultant for the power plant. “During the wintertime, we import waste.”

The garbage is burned at around 1000°C. The heat is used to heat pipes of water to create steam, which generates electricity. CO2 that would be emitted by the combustion is run through a “scrubbing system” that removes greenhouse gasses and generates more heat for power generation.

The end result is water vapor emitted through Amager Bakke’s massive smokestack. The “smoke” emits 85 per cent fewer GHGs than smoke that has not been filtered.

The generator doubles as a year-round ski hill. Designed by Bjarke Ingels Group, a Danish architecture firm, the surface is made from a slick plastic that can be skied down. A climbing wall will be opening this spring on the face of the building.

The idea is to make CopenHill “a landmark of the Danish capital,” according to project manager Lars Juel Rasmussen.

In Canada, oil and gas lifers like Howell aren’t worried about reduced demand as countries like Denmark switch to renewable and alternative forms of energy.

“I think solar and wind can play a part but to think that they're going to replace oil and gas... I just don't think it's practical,” he said.

Howell also maintains that the “ridiculous situation around pipelines” currently ongoing in Canada is one of messaging.

“I think we really have lost the public relations war at this point,” he said.

Almost immediately after getting elected, Alberta Premier Kenney announced an investment of $30 million into an "energy war room” to win the PR war Howell claims the industry has already lost. The Canadian Energy Centre, as it’s called, is staffed by policy analysts and journalists writing stories to “help raise understanding of the Canadian energy sector’s value to this country and the world, through the sharing of knowledge, facts and ideas.”

(Already, the initiative has come under fire for improper journalistic practice and copyright infringement.)

The Heavy Oil Science Centre is a place dedicated to teaching residents and tourists to Lloydminster about the history of crude oil in the region. Dexter McMillan/May 17, 2019

Lloydminster is like many other towns and cities across Alberta. Corporations like Husky solved energy problems of the 20th century for Canadians and people around the world and Canadians have benefitted from these solutions. But the world is moving on from fossil fuels, and many of the people there can’t help but feel left behind with it.

“We're in a tough spot and we need help from the federal government,” says Howell, “Albertans feel like they've turned their backs on us.”

Back at the Heavy Oil Science Centre, Squirt wants visitors to understand the importance of the fossil fuel industry to Albertans and Canadians alike.

“People used to call me a problem. A disappointment. They even called me crude,” reads a sign introducing Squirt, the drop of oil who looks like he thinks he could be cooler than you. “But now with all the people helping me, and the Upgrader where I can go to learn to be ‘refined,’ my friends say I’m very important.”

The 16-hour drive might as well be a portal from one world to another. After Calgary, mountains surge upwards from land as far as you can see in all directions. Land bridges over the highway allow for passage of wildlife from one side of the Trans-Canada Highway to another, and it’s easy to picture caribou gracefully meandering over the motorized chaos below.

Shortly after arriving, I attended an anti-pipeline rally in False Creek, a dense, affluent waterfront neighbourhood adjacent to the downtown core. The energy in Vancouver would likely feel entirely foreign to Howell, Juba and Spenrath — except, perhaps, for the anti-Trudeau sentiment.

Coun. Christine Boyle addresses a crowd of hundreds in Vancouver's False Creek. Dexter McMillan/June 9, 2019

Protesters chanted and shook their signs towards a stage, the cool blue and white of the snow-capped North Shore mountain range towering behind stacks of condos. Councillor Christine Boyle spoke over a crowd of hundreds hoisting anti-Trudeau signs or, in one case, an elaborate art installation that depicted a pipeline tied in knots.

“It’s disheartening to be doing everything that we can to meet the IPCC targets,” she told the crowd, “while the federal government undoes our efforts by encouraging the expansion and export of fossil fuels.”

The crowd groaned and swelled in support. More signs shook and heaved as agitation rippled through the attendees.

Many months after my trip to Lloydminster, just a few days after Justin Trudeau’s re-election, I checked in with Vic Juba via email. Lloydminster, after all, is a place where a Liberal party sign is a slap across the face.

“I know the election result last night was probably not what you had hoped for,” I typed before asking how he and the other residents are feeling.

“It was a sad night,” he replied a few days later. “Just found out that at the beginning of this week, Husky laid off several hundred employees that work in this area. That really hurts. Otherwise, we are doing OK.”

Lloydminster's troubles will not improve overnight, and residents know that.

“If the Trans Mountain [expansion]…were put in place today, we're still three years away from being able to get the volume going,” said Spenrath. “Frustration is the key word here.”

“Bleak,” was how a March 22 response to my post in a Facebook page called What’s Happening in Lloydminster began. I asked how folks there were fairing with COVID-19 and the economic freeze it triggered. “Scared about how long it will last. How bill’s (sic) will get paid.”

Lloydminster Mayor Gerald Aalbers agreed that the price of Western Canadian Select - “Today, it's trading at four dollars and 58 cents American,” he told me without hesitation - was concerning.

“I've been told you actually have [to] pay the pipeline company to take our oil, because it isn't fetching enough money to cover the freight costs,” he said. “That's a real difficult thing to understand for some people.”

Aalbers had a more measured, political way of addressing the federal government's response of pipelines and carbon taxes.

“There's a wait and see attitude,” he said. “This isn't going to change in one day, one week, one month, one year. This is a long-term plan.”

A 2017 report on the effects of economic downturn on Alberta is not reassuring. “The recession has left deep scars, particularly in the energy sector and across the labour market,” reads the report. "These will be slow to heal."

Lloydminster has been weathering storms and waiting for decades. The 2008 recession is still fresh on their minds heading into a situation that Mayor Aalbers calls "the perfect storm."

COVID-19 seems to have changed Trudeau’s tune somewhat, and alongside the pandemic, has brought some stimulus measures for Albertans. Alongside a more than $100 billion stimulus package announcement from Trudeau aiming to help prop up the economy through tax breaks and direct cash infusions, the prime minister announced a $15 billion bailout for the oil and gas industry, including a large fund to put Albertans to work cleaning up orphan oil wells.

A man stands waiting for the climate march in Montréal to begin. Dexter McMillan/September 27, 2019

Nobody with a stake in the future of the Canadian economy is going to get exactly what they want, that much is clear. It’s hard not to hurt with people in Lloydminster when they speak wistfully about an economy that’s putting them out of work. It’s hard not to see their ghosts and feel them when you leave, back to the parts of Canada like Montréal or Vancouver where it's harder to see the role fossil fuel extraction and processing plays in our country's prosperity.

Yet, it's easy to see the catastrophic risks we take in continuing to build an economy around fossil fuels.
